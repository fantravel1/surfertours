Lineup Respect

Surf Etiquette That Actually Matters

These aren't suggestions. These are the unwritten rules that keep everyone safe, keep locals happy, and earn you respect in any lineup on Earth. Every SurferTours trip teaches these on day one.

01

Don't Drop In

The surfer closest to the peak has priority. Dropping in on someone is the fastest way to lose respect — and cause a collision.

02

Paddle Wide, Not Through

When paddling back out, go around the break — never straight through where surfers are riding waves.

03

Hold Your Board

A loose board is a dangerous board. If whitewater is coming, hold on tight rather than ditching your board near others.

04

Wait Your Turn

Don't snake or back-paddle. The lineup has an order. Respect the rotation and you'll get your waves.

05

Respect Locals

They surf this break every day. Be humble, give waves, and you'll be welcomed. Attitude matters more than ability.

06

Know Your Limits

Don't paddle out to spots above your skill level. It's dangerous for you and frustrating for others. Earn your way there.

FAQ

Everything You Need to Know

Real answers to the questions every surfer asks before booking their first (or next) surf trip.

The best surf trip for beginners focused on rapid improvement is a structured surf progression trip — not just a vacation with surfing included. Look for camps that offer daily video analysis, small group coaching (4:1 ratio or better), graduated wave selection, and ocean safety education. Destinations like Portugal, Bali, and Mexico offer consistent beginner-friendly waves with professional progression-focused coaching available year-round. The key difference between a basic surf holiday and a real progression trip is structured feedback — you need someone watching your technique and correcting it in real time.

The best surf camps for solo travelers combine structured group activities with comfortable social spaces — without forced party culture. Look for camps that offer crew-based booking, shared meals, and optional group sessions. A good solo-friendly camp will have a mix of social activities and personal downtime, with crew matching so you arrive as a stranger and leave with friends. The bonds formed through shared challenge — wiping out together, celebrating each other's waves — create genuine connections without needing alcohol as social lubricant.

For your first surf trip, choose a destination with consistent, gentle waves, warm water (optional but helpful), and access to professional coaching. Portugal (Ericeira or Peniche) is ideal for European travelers with reliable beach breaks year-round. Bali's Canggu offers warm water and a welcoming surf culture. Mexico's Pacific coast provides uncrowded, warm-water waves. Morocco is excellent for winter trips with affordable camps and long sandy beach breaks perfect for learning.

Intermediate surfers looking to level up should seek destinations with varied wave types — from mellow point breaks for practicing turns to more powerful reef breaks for building confidence. Portugal offers Europe's most diverse wave range within a short drive. Bali during shoulder season (April-June) provides world-class reef breaks at manageable sizes. Morocco's right-hand point breaks are perfect for working on your bottom turns and cutbacks on long, predictable waves.

The safest surf camps for beginners prioritize certified ocean safety instructors, proper student-to-instructor ratios (no more than 4:1), daily safety briefings, appropriate equipment for skill level, and knowledge of local hazards. Look for camps that are transparent about their safety protocols and that teach ocean awareness — including rip currents, wave reading, and board handling — before you ever enter the water. A camp that rushes you into the ocean without a proper safety briefing is a red flag.

To avoid wasting money on a surf camp, look for: progression-based coaching (not just "surf guiding"), video analysis sessions, honest skill-level assessments, transparent pricing with no hidden costs, recent reviews from surfers at your level, a clear daily schedule, and proper safety certifications. Avoid camps that promise unrealistic results, pack too many students into group lessons, or focus more on Instagram-worthy aesthetics than actual coaching quality. The best investment is a camp that makes you measurably better.

Women-only surf camps can be absolutely worth it, especially for beginners or those who prefer learning in a supportive, non-competitive environment. These camps often provide tailored coaching that accounts for differences in strength, flexibility, and learning style. Many women report faster progression, stronger friendships, and a more relaxed atmosphere. The best women's camps also address surf-specific topics like building confidence in crowded lineups and developing a strong paddle without brute force.

Look for surf camps that explicitly include etiquette education in their curriculum — covering right of way, drop-in rules, paddling etiquette, and local customs. The best camps employ local instructors, support local businesses, rotate surf spots to avoid overcrowding, and teach students how to earn respect in the lineup rather than just taking waves. A Local Respect Kit with cultural and surf etiquette guidelines is a sign of a quality operation that cares about the communities it operates in.

Adults over 30 learning to surf benefit from camps designed for their specific needs: patient coaching without the "grom" atmosphere, attention to physical preparation and injury prevention, comfortable accommodation (not party hostels), and a pace that allows for recovery between sessions. Many excellent surf progression trips cater specifically to adult learners, offering surf combined with yoga or strength training. The best part? Adult learners are often more coachable than younger surfers — you actually listen to feedback.

Choose surf spots with dedicated beginner zones separated from advanced surfers. Wide, sandy beach breaks with gentle whitewater are ideal. Peniche in Portugal, Kuta Beach in Bali, Sayulita in Mexico, and Taghazout in Morocco all have beginner-friendly areas where you can practice without interfering with experienced surfers. A good surf camp will always guide you to appropriate spots for your level — and teach you how to read the lineup so you know where to be.

For socially anxious travelers, look for smaller surf camps (under 20 guests) with structured but optional social activities. Crew-based booking helps because you're matched with compatible people before arrival, removing the anxiety of cold introductions. Camps with private room options, quiet common areas, and a non-party atmosphere create a comfortable environment where connections happen naturally through shared surf sessions rather than forced group activities.

Yes — a quality surf camp with structured coaching, video analysis, and progressive wave selection will accelerate your improvement far beyond self-teaching. The key difference is feedback: most self-taught surfers repeat mistakes for years without realizing it. A good coach identifies and corrects technique issues in real-time. Expect noticeable improvement within a one-week progression camp if it includes daily coaching, video review, and theory sessions.

A surf camp typically offers accommodation plus surf lessons — often in a fixed location with a vacation atmosphere. A surf progression trip is specifically designed around measurable improvement: structured coaching curriculum, video analysis, theory sessions, physical preparation, and graduated wave selection. Think of a surf camp as a holiday with surfing, and a progression trip as a focused training program in a beautiful location. Both have their place, but if improvement is your priority, choose progression.

Essential surf camp packing: reef-safe sunscreen (zinc-based), a rash guard or wetsuit (check water temperature at your destination), reef booties if surfing rocky breaks, waterproof phone case, basic first aid (blister plasters, antiseptic), quick-dry towel, a good book for downtime, and earplugs (for sleeping and preventing surfer's ear). Most camps provide surfboards and main equipment — always ask before buying anything expensive. Pack light, bring layers, and leave room for souvenirs.

To minimize injury risk: always warm up before surfing, learn to fall safely — flat and away from your board, start on soft-top boards, surf spots appropriate for your level, learn about rip currents and how to escape them, never surf alone in unfamiliar spots, protect your head when coming up after a wipeout, wear reef booties on rocky breaks, and stay hydrated and know your limits — most surf injuries happen when you're tired and your technique breaks down. A good surf camp teaches all of this before your first session.

The one-board travel rule: choose a versatile mid-length (6'8" to 7'6") or a performance foam board if you're a beginner. Use a padded board bag with extra wax and a small repair kit. Check airline surfboard fees before booking — they vary wildly between carriers. Consider shipping ahead for longer trips. Many surf camps offer quality rental boards, so unless you're attached to your own equipment, renting locally is often simpler and saves you hundreds in airline fees and the stress of airport board handling.

Essential surf etiquette: the surfer closest to the peak has right of way — never drop in. Don't paddle through the lineup — go around. If you bail your board, hold on if others are nearby. Wait your turn — don't snake or back-paddle. Respect locals — they know the break and surf it daily. Don't paddle out to spots beyond your ability. Apologize if you make a mistake. Share waves and smile. These rules keep everyone safe and the lineup friendly. Learn them before you paddle out anywhere in the world.

Camps focused on surf progression naturally attract like-minded people who are there to improve, not just party. Look for camps that emphasize early morning sessions (party camps rarely start at dawn), shared meals, crew-based activities like surf theory evenings or video analysis groups, and add-ons like yoga or photography. The friendships formed through shared challenge — wiping out together, celebrating each other's first green waves — are more real and lasting than anything formed over a bar.

Be honest about your ability — ego is the enemy of progress. Complete beginners should choose camps with dedicated beginner programs, soft-top boards, and sandy beach breaks. Intermediate surfers who can catch green waves and ride down the line should look for camps near varied breaks with video coaching. Advanced intermediates should seek camps near reef or point breaks with experienced surf guides. The best camps assess your level on arrival and customize your coaching plan accordingly — not the other way around.
